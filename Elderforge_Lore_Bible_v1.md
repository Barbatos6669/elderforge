# Elderforge Lore Bible v1.0

## Purpose

This document is the source of truth for the world, story, factions, monsters, regions, magic, resources, quests, items, and tone of **Elderforge**.

Use this file when generating quests, NPCs, enemies, locations, items, dialogue, crafting materials, faction content, dungeon ideas, and worldbuilding.

Elderforge is a grounded fantasy RPG about surviving a wounded world through crafting, mastery, preparation, exploration, and faction choices.

---

# 0. Agent Instructions

When expanding Elderforge, follow these rules:

- Do not contradict this lore.
- Expand existing ideas before inventing new ones.
- Keep new content connected to gameplay.
- Every dungeon must have a reason to exist.
- Every town must have an economy.
- Every important NPC must have a goal, fear, flaw, and gameplay purpose.
- Every monster must have a habitat, behavior, weakness, and useful drops.
- Every faction must have a goal, flaw, enemy, and reason to exist.
- Every resource should matter to gathering, crafting, trade, quests, or combat.
- New lore should support crafting, gathering, combat, exploration, reputation, and player choice.
- Avoid copying lore directly from The Witcher, Albion Online, The Elder Scrolls, Dark Souls, World of Warcraft, or other fantasy games.
- Do not make the player a chosen one at the start.
- The player earns importance through actions.

Hard restrictions unless the user intentionally changes the setting:

- No guns.
- No modern technology.
- No cars.
- No airplanes.
- No sci-fi technology.
- No space travel.
- No casual superhero elements.
- No unlimited resurrection.
- No gods walking around casually.
- No joke factions that break the grounded tone.

# 1. Core Identity

## Game Title

**Elderforge**

## World Name

**Elderforge**

## One-Sentence Pitch

**Elderforge is a fantasy RPG where players survive a wounded world by mastering weapons, crafting gear, studying monsters, and forging their own path through faction conflict and ancient magic.**

## Core Theme

**Power is not given. Power is forged.**

This theme should appear everywhere:

- A sword is forged through heat and hammering.
- A village is forged through labor and trust.
- A warrior is forged through training and scars.
- A hunter is forged through knowledge and preparation.
- A kingdom is forged through law, sacrifice, and sometimes cruelty.
- The world itself was forged long ago, and now it is cracking.

## Core Fantasy

The player is not born special.

The player becomes powerful by:

- Learning the world
- Mastering weapons
- Crafting better gear
- Studying monsters
- Preparing before danger
- Building reputation
- Choosing allies carefully
- Surviving difficult choices

The world rewards knowledge, preparation, crafting, and skill more than raw character level.

# 2. Design Pillars

Elderforge should be built around these pillars:

## 1. Mastery Over Leveling

Character level gives options, but mastery creates identity.

A player who uses swords becomes better with swords.
A player who mines becomes a better miner.
A player who studies undead becomes better at fighting undead.

## 2. Equipment Defines Role

The player does not choose a permanent class.

Weapons, armor, tools, and consumables define what the player can do.

Examples:

- Sword and shield = defensive fighter
- Bow = ranged hunter
- Staff = magic user
- Heavy armor = tanky frontline role
- Leather armor = mobility and balanced combat
- Cloth armor = magic and support role

## 3. Preparation Matters

The player should prepare before dangerous encounters.

Preparation includes:

- Weapon oils
- Potions
- Food
- Bombs
- Traps
- Runes
- Resistance tonics
- Monster knowledge
- Correct gear

## 4. Crafting Is Important

Crafting is not side content. It is part of progression.

Players gather materials, refine resources, craft gear, improve equipment, trade items, and use monster parts to create specialized tools.

## 5. The World Has Consequences

Choices should matter.

Examples:

- Burning a corrupted forest may save a village but anger the Verdant Circle.
- Helping a guild may improve trade but hurt workers.
- Killing a monster may solve a contract, but studying it may reveal a bigger problem.
- Supporting the Crown may bring order but reduce local freedom.

---

# 3. Tone and Style

The tone of Elderforge should be:

- Grounded fantasy
- Dangerous but hopeful
- Mysterious but understandable
- Serious with moments of warmth
- Harsh without being hopeless

The world should include darkness, corruption, monsters, greed, death, and difficult choices.

But it should also include:

- Warm inns
- Kind villagers
- Honest workers
- Funny travelers
- Beautiful forests
- Peaceful farms
- Loyal friends
- Small acts of mercy
- Communities trying to rebuild

The world feels more real when good things are worth protecting.

Avoid making everything evil, cursed, or depressing.

# 4. World Overview

Elderforge is a medieval fantasy world filled with ruined kingdoms, dangerous wilderness, powerful guilds, old magic, corrupted monsters, and ancient ruins.

Long ago, the world was shaped by an ancient cosmic force known as **The Worldforge**.

The Worldforge is not a person, king, or normal god. It is a mythic force of creation: a cosmic forge where Stone, Flame, Water, Wind, Light, and Shadow were hammered into reality.

For thousands of years, the world was stable.

Then something cracked.

A magical disaster called **The Sundering** tore wounds into the land. These wounds are called **Rift Scars**.

Rift Scars leak unstable magic into forests, mountains, swamps, caves, ruins, and battlefields. This unstable magic corrupts animals, awakens the dead, mutates plants, empowers monsters, and damages people who use too much magic.

The modern world is rebuilding while old horrors return.

# 5. World Rules

## Technology Rules

- The world is medieval fantasy.
- Swords, bows, axes, spears, shields, staves, armor, carts, ships, and siege weapons exist.
- Blacksmithing, alchemy, farming, mining, woodworking, fishing, leatherworking, and tailoring are important.
- Basic mechanical devices may exist, such as mills, pulleys, locks, traps, cranes, water wheels, and simple clockwork.
- No modern electricity.
- No guns.
- No cars.
- No airplanes.
- No sci-fi technology.

## Magic Rules

- Magic exists, but it has a cost.
- Most people cannot use strong magic.
- Magic comes from unstable natural energy called **Ember**.
- Runes, staves, crystals, rituals, and trained focus are used to control Ember.
- Using too much magic causes physical and mental harm called **Ashburn**.
- Magic cannot solve every problem.
- Magic cannot freely resurrect the dead.
- Magic cannot create infinite resources.
- Magic cannot teleport armies across the world.
- Magic cannot make someone immortal without a terrible cost.

## Monster Rules

Monsters are usually one of these:

- Natural beasts
- Ancient creatures
- Undead
- Cursed beings
- Corrupted lifeforms
- Spirits
- Mutated animals
- Rift-born horrors

Monsters should have believable habitats and behaviors.

Monsters should drop useful materials for crafting, alchemy, cooking, tools, armor, weapons, decorations, or quests.

## Combat Rules

- Steel weapons are best against humans, bandits, animals, and normal enemies.
- Silver, rune-forged, or alchemical weapons are better against corrupted monsters, undead, spirits, and magical creatures.
- Preparation matters.
- A prepared lower-level player can defeat a stronger enemy.
- Monster knowledge should give the player a real advantage.

## Progression Rules

- Character level gives options.
- Gear determines role.
- Mastery improves performance.
- Knowledge reveals enemy weaknesses.
- Crafting supports long-term power.
- Player skill matters.

# 6. Creation Myth

In the beginning, there was only raw chaos.

From the chaos came six primal forces:

- Stone
- Flame
- Water
- Wind
- Light
- Shadow

These forces fought endlessly until they were pulled into a cosmic forge called **The Worldforge**.

The Worldforge did not speak.

It did not rule.

It simply shaped.

From Stone, it made mountains.

From Flame, it made heat, life, and hunger.

From Water, it made rivers, seas, blood, and memory.

From Wind, it made storms, breath, sound, and travel.

From Light, it made growth, truth, healing, and vision.

From Shadow, it made death, rest, secrets, and endings.

Together, these forces became Elderforge.

The first people believed the Worldforge still burned deep beneath the mountains. They built temples, cities, and kingdoms above places where they believed its heat touched the surface.

Those places became powerful.

They also became dangerous.

When mortals learned to draw power from the Worldforge, they created magic, runes, enchanted weapons, and great cities.

But they also became greedy.

Their greatest ritual cracked the ancient balance.

That disaster became known as **The Sundering**.

---

# 7. The Sundering

The Sundering is the most important historical event in Elderforge.

It happened when ancient mages, kings, and smith-priests tried to open the Worldforge and control its power directly.

They wanted to create a perfect age.

Instead, they wounded the world.

The sky burned red for seven days.

Mountains split.

Forests twisted.

The dead rose from battlefields.

Animals mutated into monsters.

Entire cities disappeared into magical storms.

The places damaged by this disaster are now called **Rift Scars**.

Rift Scars are unstable zones where monsters are stronger, resources are rarer, and magic behaves strangely.

These areas are dangerous but valuable, which makes them useful for:

- Dungeons
- Boss zones
- Rare gathering areas
- PvP zones
- World events
- Faction conflict
- High-risk crafting resources
- Main story quests

# 8. Timeline

## Age of Sparks

The earliest age.

People discovered fire, farming, stone tools, metalworking, and simple rune marks.

Small tribes formed around rivers, caves, forests, and mountains.

## Age of Kings

Kingdoms rose across Elderforge.

Roads, castles, temples, markets, guilds, and major cities were founded.

War became organized.
Trade became powerful.
Noble houses claimed land.

## Age of the Worldforge

Mages and smiths learned to combine magic and crafting.

This was the golden age of enchanted weapons, rune armor, great cities, massive bridges, magical seals, and powerful orders.

Many people now remember this age as better than it really was.

## The Sundering

Ancient rulers tried to open the Worldforge.

The ritual failed.

The world cracked.

Rift Scars appeared.

Many kingdoms collapsed.

## Age of Ash

Survivors fought monsters, famine, disease, cursed weather, and each other.

Many old roads became unsafe.

Monster hunters became necessary.

Guilds grew stronger because kings could no longer protect everyone.

## Present Day

The world is rebuilding.

Guilds are rising.
Factions are fighting for control.
Old ruins are being rediscovered.
Rift Scars are growing again.
The Hollow Choir is becoming more active.

The player begins during this unstable age.

# 9. Magic System

Magic is powered by **Ember**.

Ember is raw energy left behind by the Worldforge. It flows through the world like invisible heat.

Some places have more Ember than others:

- Ancient ruins
- Rift Scars
- Deep mines
- Sacred forests
- Volcanic mountains
- Battlefields
- Old temples
- Dragon nests
- Broken seals

## Magic Disciplines

### Flame

Fire, heat, destruction, forging, cleansing, aggression, rage, and renewal.

Common users:

- Battle mages
- Smith-priests
- Alchemists
- Cultists
- Monster hunters using fire traps

### Frost

Cold, slowing enemies, preserving materials, defense, control, silence, and endurance.

Common users:

- Mountain mages
- Wardens
- Hunters
- Stonekin rune-carvers

### Stone

Protection, armor, shields, walls, construction, endurance, weight, and stability.

Common users:

- Stonekin
- Guards
- Builders
- Heavy armor fighters
- Defensive mages

### Wind

Speed, movement, arrows, sound, storms, evasion, travel, and precision.

Common users:

- Archers
- Scouts
- Messengers
- Duelists
- Stormcoast shamans

### Grove

Plants, healing, poison, beasts, growth, decay, roots, herbs, and survival.

Common users:

- Verdant Circle
- Herbalists
- Rangers
- Healers
- Poisoners

### Light

Protection, truth, healing, undead resistance, wards, oaths, memory, and revelation.

Common users:

- Forgefaith priests
- Lantern Wardens
- Healers
- Exorcists
- Judges

### Shadow

Stealth, curses, fear, illusion, secrets, memory theft, forbidden rituals, and concealment.

Common users:

- Spies
- Assassins
- Ashen Covenant mages
- Hollow Choir cultists
- Outcasts

## Ashburn

Using too much magic causes **Ashburn**.

Ashburn can cause:

- Exhaustion
- Shaking hands
- Burn-like marks on the skin
- Memory loss
- Nightmares
- Hallucinations
- Corruption
- Emotional numbness
- Physical mutation
- Death

Most people respect magic but fear powerful mages because of Ashburn.

# 10. Cultures and Playable Peoples

For the first prototype, humans should be the main playable people. Other peoples can exist as NPCs, factions, or future playable options.

## Humans

Humans are the most common people in Elderforge.

They are adaptable, ambitious, divided, and creative.

Humans build kingdoms, guilds, farms, ports, roads, castles, and armies.

Humans can be:

- Warriors
- Crafters
- Merchants
- Mages
- Hunters
- Farmers
- Guards
- Bandits
- Nobles
- Priests
- Mercenaries

Human strengths:

- Adaptability
- Social organization
- Trade
- Crafting
- Expansion

Human flaws:

- Greed
- Short memory
- Political division
- Reckless ambition

## Stonekin

Stonekin are mountain-born people with dense bodies, rough skin patterns, and strong traditions of mining, smithing, and oath-keeping.

They believe every soul has a shape, and life is the process of hammering that shape into something worthy.

They are known for:

- Mining
- Blacksmithing
- Rune carving
- Heavy armor
- Axes and hammers
- Mountain cities
- Stone architecture

They value:

- Patience
- Craft
- Oaths
- Endurance
- Ancestors
- Useful work

They dislike:

- Waste
- Broken promises
- Careless magic
- Weak tools
- Empty boasting

## Groveborn

Groveborn are forest people connected to ancient trees and natural magic.

They are not immortal, but they live longer than humans.

They value memory, balance, seasons, and survival.

They are known for:

- Herbalism
- Bows
- Nature magic
- Beast knowledge
- Forest settlements
- Healing
- Poison craft
- Hidden paths

They distrust cities that cut too deeply into old forests.

Groveborn culture is built around memory. Important stories are carved into living trees, sung during seasonal rites, or passed through family lines.

## Ashmarked

Ashmarked are people whose ancestors survived heavy exposure to Rift magic.

They often have unusual eyes, dark veins, pale skin markings, faint glowing scars, or strange resistance to Ember.

Many people fear them because they are associated with corruption, but not all Ashmarked are evil.

They are known for:

- Magic sensitivity
- Resistance to some corruption
- Outsider communities
- Shadow and flame magic
- Monster knowledge
- Strange dreams

Some become heroes.
Some become villains.
Most are just trying to survive.

# 11. Major Factions

The Lantern Wardens are monster hunters, investigators, alchemists, trackers, and protectors of roads and villages.

They carry lanterns filled with alchemical light that reveals tracks, curses, spirit traces, and corruption.

Hunt monsters, study corruption, protect settlements, and maintain bestiary archives.

- Every curse has a source.

- Gathering resources
- Crafting basic items
- Repairing gear

- Tracking monsters

# 14. Starter Town: Hearthbridge

Hearthbridge is the first major settlement.

It sits beside an old stone bridge crossing the Emberwash River.

The town survives through farming, fishing, lumber, mining, trade, and repair work for caravans.

The bridge is older than the town. Some say it was built during the Age of the Worldforge.

## Important Locations

### The Hammer & Hearth

The main inn.

Players can find rumors, contracts, food, travelers, resting options, and early story NPCs here.

### Old Marra's Forge

### The Green Cup

A small herbalist shop.

Used for potions, herbs, monster oils, antidotes, food additives, and preparation tutorials.

### The Notice Board

Used for contracts, side quests, monster hunts, gathering requests, faction tasks, and rumors.

### The Training Yard

- Tool repair

# 16. Resources and Materials

Resources should connect gathering, crafting, economy, and lore.

## Iron Ore

Common metal used for basic weapons, tools, nails, armor, traps, and repairs.

Found in Hearthvale, Frostspine, caves, and small mines.

## Copper Ore

Soft metal used for basic tools, fittings, wires, decorative pieces, alchemical devices, and low-tier crafting.

Found in Hearthvale hills and shallow mines.

## Silver Ore

Valuable metal used against undead, spirits, and corrupted monsters.

Found mostly in Frostspine Mountains.

Silver should be rare enough that early players must earn or craft silver weapons carefully.

## Oak Wood

Common strong wood used for bows, shields, buildings, handles, carts, and basic tools.

Found in Hearthvale.

## Ironwood

A dense magical wood used for bows, shields, staves, reinforced doors, and strong building materials.

Found in Blackroot Forest.

## Silverneedle Pine

A pale mountain tree whose wood resists magical corruption.

Used for staves, bows, anti-monster tools, warding stakes, and lantern frames.

Found in Frostspine Mountains.

## Moonleaf

A faintly glowing herb used in healing potions, anti-poison mixtures, monster oils, and calming tonics.

Found in forests and moonlit groves.

## Blackroot Sap

A thick black resin gathered from corrupted trees.

Dangerous in raw form, but useful in poisons, traps, monster oils, and corruption research.

## Emberstone

Crystallized raw magic.

Used for runes, enchanted gear, staff crafting, rituals, and risky magical upgrades.

Found near Rift Scars.

## Stormglass

Glass-like material formed when lightning strikes magically charged sand or coastal stone.

Used for lightning magic, lenses, traps, signal devices, and storm-themed gear.

## Wyrdbone

Bone from corrupted monsters.

Used for dark alchemy, forbidden crafting, monster bait, ritual tools, and dangerous upgrades.

## Grave Moss

A pale moss that grows near old graves, ruins, and undead sites.

Used in spirit potions, warding circles, sleep tonics, and undead oils.

# 17. Monster Families

Every monster family should support the monster knowledge system.

Knowledge should reveal behavior, drops, weaknesses, and special tactics.

## Wolves

Normal wolves are common in forests and hills.

Corrupted wolves are more dangerous and aggressive.

### Habitat

Forests, hills, snowy regions, abandoned farms.

### Behavior

Hunt in packs, surround prey, test weak targets, retreat when badly injured unless corrupted.

### Weaknesses

- Fire
- Traps
- Bleeding effects
- Loud noise
- Separating pack members

### Drops

- Hide
- Meat
- Teeth
- Claws
- Corrupted fang from corrupted variants

### Knowledge Unlocks

- Knowledge 10: Wolves hunt in packs.
- Knowledge 25: Fire scares normal wolves.
- Knowledge 50: Corrupted wolves ignore fear.
- Knowledge 75: Alpha wolves can call reinforcements.
- Knowledge 100: Small bonus damage against wolf-type enemies.

---

## Bandits

Bandits are desperate people, criminals, deserters, or failed mercenaries who prey on roads and villages.

Not every bandit is evil, but most are dangerous.

### Habitat

Roads, abandoned camps, ruined towers, forests, caves, old farms.

### Behavior

Ambush travelers, flee if outmatched, use traps, target weak caravans, sometimes negotiate.

### Weaknesses

- Fear
- Disarming
- Fire near camps
- Cutting off escape routes
- Breaking morale

### Drops

- Coins
- Food
- Crude weapons
- Leather scraps
- Stolen goods
- Notes revealing larger threats

### Knowledge Unlocks

- Knowledge 10: Bandits often use ambush points near roads.
- Knowledge 25: Campfires and tracks reveal camp size.
- Knowledge 50: Leaders often carry stolen contract notes.
- Knowledge 75: Some groups are backed by corrupt nobles or guilds.
- Knowledge 100: Improved intimidation or negotiation options against bandits.

---

## Undead

Undead are corpses animated by unstable Ember, curses, unfinished death rituals, or Rift energy.

### Habitat

Graveyards, battlefields, swamps, ruins, crypts, mass graves.

### Behavior

Slow but persistent. Some are mindless. Others remember fragments of life.

### Weaknesses

- Silver
- Light magic
- Fire
- Blessed oil
- Destroying the head
- Resolving the source of the curse

### Drops

- Bone
- Grave dust
- Rusted gear
- Spirit residue
- Cursed jewelry
- Grave moss

### Knowledge Unlocks

- Knowledge 10: Undead are drawn to sound.
- Knowledge 25: Silver damages undead better than iron.
- Knowledge 50: Light magic weakens them.
- Knowledge 75: Some undead are bound to objects.
- Knowledge 100: Reveal undead anchor points.

---

## Trolls

Trolls are large territorial creatures that live in mountains, caves, old bridges, and ruins.

Not all trolls are evil. Some can speak, trade, or guard places.

### Habitat

Mountains, caves, bridges, ruins, deep ravines.

### Behavior

Territorial, stubborn, strong, sometimes intelligent, sometimes lonely.

### Weaknesses

- Poison
- Eye attacks
- Heavy traps
- Frost magic
- Exhaustion tactics
- Attacking joints

### Drops

- Troll hide
- Troll fat
- Heavy bones
- Rare teeth
- Stone-like skin plates

### Knowledge Unlocks

- Knowledge 10: Trolls are territorial.
- Knowledge 25: Some trolls can be reasoned with.
- Knowledge 50: Poison slows troll regeneration.
- Knowledge 75: Troll armor is weakest near joints.
- Knowledge 100: Unlock special anti-troll trap recipe.

---

## Wraiths

Wraiths are spirits trapped by grief, curses, murder, betrayal, or Rift magic.

### Habitat

Ruins, battlefields, abandoned homes, graveyards, cursed objects.

### Behavior

Phase through objects, attack at night, repeat memories from life, guard places or objects.

### Weaknesses

- Silver
- Light magic
- Warding circles
- Spirit oil
- Resolving unfinished business

### Drops

- Spirit dust
- Ectoplasm
- Cold ash
- Cursed fragments
- Memory shards

### Gameplay Note

Some wraiths should be defeated through investigation, truth, burial, or object cleansing instead of only combat.

---

## Rootbound

Rootbound are plants, animals, corpses, or structures taken over by corrupted roots.

### Habitat

Blackroot Forest, old groves, abandoned farms, corrupted orchards.

### Behavior

Ambush prey, spread corruption, hide as normal plants, protect root hearts.

### Weaknesses

- Fire
- Axes
- Purifying oils
- Cutting root hearts
- Sunlight rituals

### Drops

- Blackroot sap
- Twisted bark
- Thorn fibers
- Poison glands
- Root hearts

---

## Drakes

Drakes are smaller dragon-like creatures.

True dragons are nearly extinct, but drakes remain.

### Habitat

Mountains, cliffs, volcanic caves, old ruins, high nests.

### Behavior

Predatory, territorial, intelligent for beasts, attracted to heat and shiny objects.

### Weaknesses

- Piercing weapons
- Frost
- Wing damage
- Poisoned bait
- Traps near nests

### Drops

- Scales
- Claws
- Teeth
- Drake leather
- Ember glands

# 24. Faction Quest Design

Faction quests should create tension.

The player should often be able to help one group while annoying another.

Examples:

- Help the Ironbound Guild expand a mine, but anger the Verdant Circle.
- Help the Verdant Circle protect a sacred grove, but reduce Hearthbridge lumber supply.
- Help the Crown restore road patrols, but increase taxes on local merchants.
- Help the Lantern Wardens expose a curse, but reveal a secret the town wanted buried.
- Help the Ashen Covenant study a Rift Scar, but risk spreading corruption.

Factions should not be purely good or evil, except the Hollow Choir can be the clearest villain faction.

Even then, some Hollow Choir members may be tragic, manipulated, or desperate.

# 25. Dialogue Style

Dialogue should be readable and character-driven.

Avoid huge lore dumps.

NPCs should speak based on their background.

## Dialogue Rules

- Keep most lines short.
- Let personality show through word choice.
- Use lore naturally.
- Give the player clear choices.
- Avoid modern slang unless it fits a specific comedic character.
- Do not make every NPC sound poetic.
- Do not make every NPC explain the world like a textbook.

## Example Dialogue

### Captain Mara Venn

"Road's been quiet for two days. That's usually when something ugly is thinking."

### Old Marra

"A blade doesn't care about your courage. It cares if you kept the edge clean."

### Sella Brightcup

"Drink this before the bite turns black. And next time, try not getting bitten."

### Brother Caldus Wren

"The dead do not always want vengeance. Sometimes they only want someone to remember their names."

### Veyra Ash-Eyed

"People see these scars and think they know my story. They don't. Try not to become like them."

# 26. Progression Lore Connection

The lore should support the progression system.

## Character Level

Represents broad experience, reputation, and personal growth.

It should not be the only source of power.

## Equipment Tier

Represents material quality, craftsmanship, and access to better resources.

High-tier gear should require dangerous gathering, crafting mastery, rare monster parts, or faction access.

## Weapon Mastery

Represents practice with a weapon family.

A sword user becomes stronger by using swords.
A bow user becomes stronger by using bows.

## Armor Mastery

Represents learning how to move, block, dodge, and survive in specific armor types.

## Monster Knowledge

Represents studying enemies through combat, tracking, books, NPCs, and investigation.

Knowledge should reveal weaknesses, drops, habits, and preparation tools.

## Crafting Mastery

Represents long-term skill in making useful things.

Crafters should matter to the world.

# 28. Main Story Direction

The main story begins small.

The player is not immediately saving the world.

At first, the player helps villages, learns trades, hunts monsters, gathers resources, crafts gear, and gains reputation.

Over time, the player discovers that Rift Scars are growing stronger.

The Hollow Choir is trying to reopen the Worldforge.

The Lantern Wardens are too weak to stop them alone.

The Ironbound Guild wants to mine Rift zones for profit.

The Ashen Covenant wants to study the Rift, even if it risks disaster.

The Verdant Circle wants to seal corrupted regions, even if towns must be abandoned.

The Crown of Valewick wants to control everything in the name of order.

The player must decide who to support, who to oppose, and what kind of world Elderforge should become.

# 29. Main Lore Summary for Agents

Elderforge is a grounded fantasy RPG set in a wounded world damaged by an ancient magical disaster called The Sundering. The land is scarred by unstable magical zones called Rift Scars, which create monsters, undead, rare resources, and dangerous ruins. The player is not a chosen hero. Power comes from crafting, equipment, mastery, monster knowledge, preparation, and faction choices. Major factions include the Lantern Wardens, Ironbound Guild, Verdant Circle, Ashen Covenant, Crown of Valewick, Free Companies, and Hollow Choir. The starting region is Hearthvale, centered around the town of Hearthbridge. The main theme is: Power is not given. Power is forged.

# 30. Codex Agent Build Instructions

When using this lore to generate game content, follow this order:

1. Start with Hearthvale and Hearthbridge.
2. Create only a small playable slice first.
3. Use the starter NPCs before inventing new major NPCs.
4. Use wolves, bandits, undead, and corrupted wolves before adding advanced monsters.
5. Use Lantern Wardens, Ironbound Guild, Verdant Circle, and Crown of Valewick before adding distant factions.
6. Connect quests to gameplay systems.
7. Connect resources to crafting recipes.
8. Connect monsters to knowledge unlocks.
9. Connect locations to economy and history.
10. Keep all content expandable.

The first goal is not to build the whole world.

The first goal is to make Hearthvale feel alive.
